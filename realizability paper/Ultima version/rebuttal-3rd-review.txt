Regarding the point about invariants, we agree that the properties highlighted in the example are not new per se. The purpose of this example is rather to show that such properties can be reflected at the type level in our system, thanks to the explicit tracking of bases.

More precisely, the example illustrates that a naive formulation of the program leads to a coarse typing involving #, whereas a basis-aware reformulation yields a strictly more precise type that captures the deterministic behaviour of the algorithm. In particular, the typing makes explicit that the result is classical, and allows one to eliminate unnecessary use of #-types. This refinement is not available in previous approaches where the basis is fixed, even when the invariants are known. We will revise the presentation to make this point clearer and avoid suggesting that the goal is to discover new invariants.

With respect to measurement, it is not included in the language; however, adding measurement is not a problem per se. Many related languages (e.g. [9, 11]) include it, and its integration into our setting is straightforward, but comes at the cost of a significant increase in complexity. Since our focus is on basis sensitivity and its interaction with linearity, we chose to keep the calculus simple and leave measurement aside.

The purpose of the teleportation example is therefore not to model the communication aspects of the protocol, but to illustrate how the deferred-measurement principle can be expressed within the calculus. In particular, the example highlights how the decomposition of a state in the Bell basis induces a corresponding decomposition of the computation into orthogonal branches, each associated with a unitary correction. The encoding makes this structure explicit, and the type system ensures that each branch is well-typed and unitary, and that the overall term has the expected type. We will clarify this point in the revised version to better align the example with its intended purpose.
