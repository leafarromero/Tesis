Reviewer #2

> One can potentially use this lambda calculus to describe quantum computations, but one can also use it to describe non-unitary maps or other non-physical things.

Our calculus builds on Lineal [1], which is untyped and allows Church encodings of booleans, with linear combinations representing qubits. In our simply typed setting, we introduce the constants |0⟩ and |1⟩, as Church encodings would require polymorphism.

The characterisation of unitary maps in [10] was restricted to the computational basis. In the present work, we extend this result to arbitrary bases, yielding a significantly stronger characterisation.

As future work, we plan to develop a polymorphic version to recover Church encodings and obtain an even more general characterisation.

Furthermore, all well-typed terms in our system have norm 1, and well-typed abstractions preserve this norm. Although not included in the current version, we could also make explicit that orthogonality is preserved, implying that well-typed abstractions correspond to isometries.

> The calculus provides a notion of superposition of functions, but it is not clear that it is a good notion. [...]

A combination of abstractions has an arrow type only when it behaves appropriately, namely when it preserves the norm. This is enforced by the realizability semantics and by the typing rules, which requires that the corresponding linear combination of bodies is well-typed.

In contrast, the examples given are typable as ♯ of an arrow type, rather than as arrow types themselves. They are treated as vectors in a vector space, not as functions. Then, it is expected that v1 − v1 has norm 0, v1 + v1 has norm 2, etc., which is consistent with their interpretation as vectors rather than functions.
